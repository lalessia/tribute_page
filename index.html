Ada Lovelace

The mother of Information Technology

Biography














Date Event
10/12/1815 Ada Lovelace was born in London by Lord Byron and Anne Isabella
1828 At the age of 12 she tried to build a pair of wings that allowed her to soar in flight and escape from her home. She hypothesized which materials to use in the construction and what size the wings had to support her weight, she studied the anatomy of the birds to understand the proportions between wings and the rest of the body, she also wrote a book on the subject entitled Flylogy.
05/06/1833 She met Charles Babbage (author of the differential machine: difference engine)
08/07/1835 Ada married William King-Noel, count of Lovelace
12/05/1836 The first child was born: Byron
1837 Ada supported the new Babbage's project: ‘the analytic machine’ and she started a partnership with him.
22/09/1837 The second child was born: Anne Isabella.
02/07/1839 The third child was born: Ralph Gordon.
1841 She discovered that Lord Byron was his father.
1842 The engineer Luigi Federico Menabrea wrote an article on the analytical machine of Babbage. Babbage proposed to Ada to translate it, allowing her to add some personal notes.
1843 She translation of the Membrane article is published in 'scientific memoirs'. The result was a series of illuminating notes on the purpose of the machine and on the limits of artificial intelligence.
27/11/1852 She dies at the age of 37.
1979: A programming language is called as her: ADA.

Aphorisms

That brain of mine is something more than merely mortal, as time will show.

Imagination is the Discovering Faculty, pre-eminently. It is that which penetrates into the unseen worlds around us, the worlds of Science.

Those who have learned to walk on the threshold of the unknown worlds, by means of what are commonly termed par excellence the exact sciences, may then, with the fair white wings of imagination, hope to soar further into the unexplored amidst which we live.

I am never so happy as when I am really engaged in good earnest, & it makes me must wonderfully cheerful & merry at other times, which is curious & very satisfactory

Mathematical science shows what is. It is the language of unseen relations between things. But to use and apply that language, we must be able fully to appreciate, to feel, to seize the unseen, the unconscious.

I am much pleased to find how very well I stand work & how my powers of attention & continued effort increase.

I believe myself to possess a most singular combination of qualities exactly fitted to make me pre-eminently a discoverer of the hidden realities of nature.

We may say most aptly that the Analytical Engine weaves algebraical patterns just as the Jacquard loom weaves flowers and leaves.

Those who incline to very strictly utilitarian views may perhaps feel that the peculiar powers of the Analytical Engine bear upon questions of abstract and speculative science rather than upon those involving everyday and ordinary human interests.
